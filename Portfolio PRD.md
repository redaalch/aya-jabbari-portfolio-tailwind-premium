1. Product Summary

This product is a polished, single-page personal portfolio website for Aya Jabbari, a 4th-year Computer Engineering student at ENSA Fès based in Fès, Morocco.

The portfolio should position Aya as a credible early-career computer engineer with practical experience in full-stack development, applied AI, data-oriented systems, automation, and software engineering foundations.

The website should help recruiters, internship coordinators, technical reviewers, and collaborators quickly understand who Aya is, what she can build, which technologies she uses, and why she is a strong candidate for internships, PFA opportunities, junior technical roles, hackathons, and technical collaborations.

The portfolio should feel elegant, warm, professional, and technical. The visual direction should use a soft cream, pink, and plum palette, serif headings, modern cards, clean spacing, and a polished but human aesthetic.

The site must stay honest to Aya’s real CV. It should not invent experience, results, metrics, links, awards, production deployments, or senior-level claims.

3. Target Audience

The primary audience is recruiters, hiring managers, and internship coordinators looking for interns or junior profiles in software development, full-stack engineering, backend development, data, applied AI, automation, or technical consulting.

The secondary audience is technical reviewers, engineers, PFA supervisors, hackathon evaluators, and academic mentors who want to assess Aya’s real technical depth through project evidence.

The tertiary audience is professional communities, IEEE members, hackathon scouts, student clubs, collaborators, and peers who may invite Aya to participate in projects, competitions, or technical initiatives.

The site should be readable by non-technical recruiters while still giving enough implementation detail for engineers.

Out of scope as a primary audience: senior-level recruiters, freelance clients, B2B buyers, and startup customers.

6. Core User Journeys
Journey A — Recruiter on mobile, 60–90 seconds

The recruiter lands on the hero section, reads Aya’s role and positioning, sees that she is open to internships, scans the skills strip, notices ONDA and the AI hackathons, opens one project, then taps email or downloads the CV.

Design implication: the page must be mobile-first, fast, and CTA-driven.

Journey B — Technical reviewer on desktop, 5–8 minutes

The reviewer skips directly to projects, checks project cards, scans stack tags, opens two or three project details, checks education and certifications, then opens GitHub or the CV.

Design implication: project cards must include architecture, stack, role, and implementation scope.

Journey C — Hackathon scout or program coordinator, 30 seconds

The visitor looks for proof of Ramadan AI and Capgemini Hackathon participation, then shares the portfolio internally.

Design implication: hackathons should be a dedicated section, not buried inside projects.

Journey D — PFA supervisor or professor, 2 minutes

The visitor reads the profile, checks education, scans project breadth, notices volunteering and IEEE/community involvement, and evaluates whether Aya is serious and well-rounded.

Design implication: education and volunteering should support credibility without overpowering technical content.

Journey E — Collaborator or peer

The visitor wants to understand Aya’s interests and technical strengths, then contacts her for a project, competition, or community activity.

Design implication: the site should feel approachable, not only corporate.

13. UX Principles

The portfolio should be easy to scan and fast to understand.

Core UX principles:

Mobile-first design. Many recruiters will open the site from LinkedIn or email.

Clear visual hierarchy. Hero, ONDA, hackathons, projects, and skills should appear before secondary details.

One main CTA per viewport. Do not overload each section with too many competing buttons.

Card-based layout. Projects, certifications, skills, and hackathons should be structured as modern cards.

Readable technical content. Recruiters should understand the purpose of each project without needing deep engineering knowledge.

Progressive disclosure. Show short project summaries first, then optional detail sections or case-study modals.

No skill bars. Use grouped badges or categorized lists.

Accessible colors. Cream, pink, and plum must maintain readable contrast.

Subtle animation only. No animation should delay access to content.

Fast performance. Avoid heavy hero videos, unoptimized images, or unnecessary scripts.

Sticky navigation. Keep section anchors easily accessible.

Honest empty states. If there is no GitHub or live demo, say “Code available on request” or omit the button.

Print-friendly summary. The page should print cleanly or export easily as a one-page profile if needed.

It should not present Aya as a senior engineer, AI researcher, DevOps engineer, data scientist, or cloud architect.

It should not invent awards, metrics, production deployments, repository links, testimonials, or company endorsements.

It should not make volunteering visually larger than technical experience and projects.

16. Risks If the Portfolio Exaggerates the CV

Exaggerating Aya’s profile would damage credibility.

Main risks:

Recruiters may ask interview questions based on inflated claims, creating a mismatch.

Technical reviewers may lose trust if project descriptions sound more advanced than the implementation.

Calling Aya an “AI expert,” “senior engineer,” or “cloud architect” would conflict with her current student status.

Invented metrics such as “reduced processing time by 60%” could be challenged during interviews.

Claiming hackathon wins, rankings, or finalist status without confirmation would be risky.

Presenting ONDA work as a long-term production deployment without confirmation could misrepresent the internship.

Using Capgemini, ONDA, Oracle, or Ministry logos in a way that implies endorsement could create legal or credibility issues.

AI-generated project screenshots that do not reflect the real project could harm trust if a recruiter asks for the repo.

The safest and strongest strategy is honest specificity. Aya already has enough credible material without exaggeration.
